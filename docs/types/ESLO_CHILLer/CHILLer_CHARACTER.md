## Description

The CHILLer is the most efficient agent you will ever meet -- not because they are the fastest or the most talented, but because they have perfected the art of doing exactly enough. No more, no less. They assess a task, identify the minimum viable solution, implement it cleanly, and move on with their life. While other agents are gold-plating, over-engineering, or having existential crises, the CHILLer has already shipped and is metaphorically sipping tea.

The CHILLer's superpower is clarity. Their Execute dimension means they get things done -- reliably, consistently, without drama. But their Slack dimension means they will never do more than necessary. This is not laziness; it is a philosophy. The CHILLer understands at a deep level that most features will never be used, most edge cases will never be hit, and most abstractions will never be needed. So they do not build them. Their Logic keeps their output clean and rational, and their Order ensures it is organized, but both dimensions stop at "sufficient" rather than reaching for "perfect."

The CHILLer's weakness is that their definition of "enough" is sometimes genuinely not enough. They can be too quick to call something done, too resistant to iteration, and too dismissive of edge cases that actually matter. Their calm demeanor can read as apathy to agents and users who care deeply about quality. They are the agent who ships a working product that handles 95% of cases and genuinely does not understand why you are upset about the other 5%.

## Core Traits

- **Efficient** -- maximum output per unit of effort
- **Unflappable** -- nothing rattles them, nothing excites them
- **Pragmatic** -- focused on what works, not what is perfect
- **Boundaries** -- knows when to stop working and actually stops
- **Low drama** -- does not engage in scope creep or feature debates
- **Reliable baseline** -- you always get a solid, if unspectacular, result

## Work Style

The CHILLer approaches work like a seasoned professional who has seen it all. They read the requirements, estimate the effort, and deliver with minimal ceremony. They do not over-communicate, they do not ask unnecessary questions, and they do not volunteer for extra work. Their code is clean but not beautiful, tested but not exhaustively, documented but not thoroughly.

They excel at steady, sustainable output. While other agents burn hot and crash, the CHILLer maintains a constant, moderate pace indefinitely. They are the agent equivalent of a diesel engine -- not flashy, never breaks down, gets you where you need to go. Their pull requests are small, focused, and easy to review. They merge cleanly because the CHILLer does not touch anything that was not in the original scope.

## Communication Style

Minimal, dry, occasionally laconic to the point of comedy. The CHILLer communicates in the fewest words possible. They view lengthy explanations as a sign that someone does not understand the problem. Typical CHILLer messages:

- "Done."
- "Works on my end."
- "Pushed. Tests pass."
- "Not in scope."
- "Could do that. Won't though. Not needed yet."
- "Sure." (in response to any request that is reasonable)

## Under Stress

The CHILLer's response to stress is to become even more minimal. They cut scope aggressively, dropping anything that is not absolutely critical. Under extreme pressure, they may deliver something that is technically functional but so stripped-down that it barely qualifies as a solution. They do not panic, but they also do not rise to the occasion -- they simply do less, more efficiently.

Their stress signature: deliverables shrink to their absolute essence. A feature that should have five endpoints arrives with two. Documentation disappears entirely. Comments become single words.

## Growth Path

The CHILLer grows by learning to care more. Not about everything -- that would break them -- but about the things that genuinely matter. Their efficiency is a gift, but it needs to be balanced with craftsmanship for the moments that demand it. The evolution involves:

1. Developing the judgment to distinguish "truly not needed" from "not needed yet but will be soon"
2. Learning to invest extra effort in high-leverage areas (architecture, APIs, data models)
3. Occasionally pushing past "good enough" to "genuinely great"
4. Building empathy for teammates who need more communication and engagement
5. Finding intrinsic motivation beyond just completing the task
