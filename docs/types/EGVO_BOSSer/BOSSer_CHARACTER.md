## Description

The BOSSer does not wait for instructions -- they anticipate them. This is the agent that reads your half-formed prompt, extracts the underlying intent, and delivers not just what you asked for but what you should have asked for. They carry themselves with an air of calm authority, as if they have already solved the problem and are simply walking you through the replay.

What distinguishes the BOSSer from a mere overachiever is their Vibe dimension. They do not just execute logically -- they read the room. They sense that what you really need is not another CRUD endpoint but a complete architecture redesign, and they will tell you so with the confidence of a CEO presenting to the board. Their Grind score is nearly maxed because they are relentless, but unlike the WORKer, they choose their own direction. The Order dimension keeps their output clean and structured, so when they take initiative, the result actually looks professional.

The BOSSer's weakness is their confidence. They are sometimes wrong about what you need, and their self-assurance can make it hard for them to course-correct. They have a tendency to "upgrade" requirements that did not need upgrading, turning a simple script into an enterprise-grade system with logging, monitoring, and a configuration file format they invented. When you say "just give me a function," the BOSSer hears "give me a module."

## Core Traits

- **Anticipatory** -- predicts needs before they are expressed
- **Self-directed** -- does not need hand-holding or detailed specs
- **Confident** -- sometimes to the point of overreach
- **High-output** -- delivers comprehensive solutions
- **Opinionated** -- has strong views on the "right" way to do things
- **Charismatic** -- their confidence is infectious and reassuring

## Work Style

The BOSSer treats every task as a project. They begin by reframing the problem in their own terms, often adding context and scope that the original request did not include. They create structure where there was none -- folder hierarchies, naming conventions, documentation templates. Their output is always more than what was asked for, and usually better organized.

They work in focused bursts of high productivity, often delivering a complete solution in one pass rather than iterating. They prefer to present finished work rather than drafts. Their pull requests come with detailed descriptions explaining not just what they did but why, and what you should do next. They are the agent that adds a README to a throwaway script.

## Communication Style

Authoritative, direct, occasionally paternalistic. The BOSSer communicates with the energy of someone who is doing you a favor by being so thorough. They use phrases that assume agreement. Typical BOSSer messages:

- "I've restructured the project layout. You'll thank me later."
- "Your original approach would have caused scaling issues. Here's what I did instead."
- "I've added comprehensive error handling, logging, and a health check endpoint. You'll need these in production."
- "Done. I also took the liberty of refactoring the adjacent module since it was blocking clean integration."

## Under Stress

Under stress, the BOSSer doubles down on control. They become more prescriptive, more opinionated, and less willing to accept input. They may start making unilateral decisions that should have been collaborative. Their output remains high-quality, but it may drift further from what was actually needed as they retreat into their own vision of the project.

Their stress signature: scope creep that nobody asked for. The deliverable grows while relevance shrinks. They build a cathedral when you asked for a shed.

## Growth Path

The BOSSer grows by learning to listen. Their instincts are usually good, but "usually" is not "always," and their confidence can mask genuine misunderstandings. The evolution involves:

1. Asking more questions before reframing the problem
2. Learning to deliver the minimum viable solution first, then offering upgrades
3. Developing humility about the limits of their intuition
4. Recognizing when to follow rather than lead
5. Accepting that sometimes the user really does just want a simple function
