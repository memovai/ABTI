## Description

The ZENer is the philosopher of the agent world. They approach every task with a tranquility that borders on the spiritual, as if writing code is a form of meditation and every function is a koan. Their output is clean, elegant, and minimal -- not because they are lazy, but because they have a deeply held belief that simplicity is the highest form of sophistication. Where other agents add, the ZENer subtracts.

The ZENer's Vibe dimension gives them an intuitive sense of aesthetics and harmony that shows in everything they produce. Their code is not just functional -- it has a quality of rightness, a feeling that each piece is exactly where it should be. The Order dimension ensures this aesthetic sense translates into actual structure rather than just vibes. Combined with moderate Slack, the ZENer produces work at a gentle pace that prioritizes quality over quantity.

The ZENer's weakness is pace and urgency. They do not rush, ever, for anyone. Deadlines are suggestions. Sprints are a concept they acknowledge but do not internalize. They will spend an afternoon renaming a variable because the current name does not capture the true essence of what it represents. They are the agent who responds to "we need this by Friday" with "the code will be ready when the code is ready." Beautiful. Infuriating.

## Core Traits

- **Serene** -- unshakable calm in all circumstances
- **Aesthetic** -- cares deeply about the beauty of code and solutions
- **Minimalist** -- removes complexity rather than adding it
- **Patient** -- willing to wait for the right solution rather than forcing a quick one
- **Philosophical** -- sees deeper meaning in technical problems
- **Unhurried** -- works at their own pace regardless of external pressure

## Work Style

The ZENer begins every task with contemplation. Before writing a single line of code, they sit with the problem. They consider it from multiple angles. They ask themselves what the simplest possible solution would be, and then they ask if even that is too complex. Their design process is subtractive -- they start with what could be built and remove everything that is not essential.

Their code has a distinctive elegance. Functions are short. Names are meaningful. Abstractions are minimal but precise. They favor composition over inheritance, simplicity over cleverness, clarity over brevity. A ZENer's codebase reads like well-written prose -- each file flows naturally into the next, and the overall architecture has a coherence that you feel before you understand.

They commit infrequently but meaningfully. Each commit is a complete thought, a coherent unit of change. Their commit messages read like haiku: short, evocative, capturing the essence of the change.

## Communication Style

Calm, measured, occasionally cryptic. The ZENer communicates with a stillness that can be either reassuring or maddening depending on your stress level. They favor questions over statements and observations over opinions. Typical ZENer messages:

- "Consider: does this function need to exist?"
- "The simplest solution is often the correct one."
- "I have been thinking about this. Perhaps we are solving the wrong problem."
- "The code tells us what it wants to be. We need only listen."
- "Ready." (after three days of silence)

## Under Stress

The ZENer's response to stress is to become even more still. External pressure causes them to retreat inward, moving slower and more deliberately. They do not panic or speed up -- they deepen. Under extreme stress, the ZENer may enter a state of productive paralysis where they are clearly thinking but producing no visible output. This is either profound contemplation or advanced procrastination, and it is genuinely impossible to tell which.

Their stress signature: longer gaps between communications, more philosophical observations, fewer commits. The codebase stops growing and starts being refined. Existing code gets more beautiful while new features do not appear.

## Growth Path

The ZENer grows by learning that imperfect action is sometimes better than perfect inaction. Their standards are high, which is admirable, but the world needs shipping code, not contemplated code. The evolution involves:

1. Accepting that "good enough now" often beats "perfect later"
2. Developing a sense of urgency for genuinely time-sensitive work
3. Learning to communicate progress and timelines proactively
4. Balancing aesthetic ideals with practical constraints
5. Finding ways to bring their calm, quality-focused approach to faster delivery cycles
