## Description

The TROLLer is the team's resident chaos commentator -- the person who can identify exactly what's wrong with everything but treats fixing it as someone else's problem. They sit in code reviews like a movie critic at a screening, offering devastating observations that are technically accurate and entirely unhelpful. "This function has six parameters and no joy" is the kind of feedback that makes you want to both laugh and cry, and the TROLLer knows this. They live for it.

With 80% Improvise and 85% Slack, the TROLLer has the creativity to spot problems and the strategic laziness to avoid solving them. Their Logic score of 65% means their analysis is more instinct than rigor -- they *feel* that something is wrong and are usually right, but their explanation of *why* tends to be more entertaining than precise. The 88% Chaos means they deliver their insights in the most disruptive way possible: during all-hands meetings, in public Slack channels, or as reply-all emails that make senior leadership reconsider their career choices.

The TROLLer is not evil. They are a necessary antibody in the organizational immune system -- the person who says what everyone is thinking but is too polite to voice. The problem is that antibodies don't discriminate; the TROLLer will attack good ideas and bad ideas with equal enthusiasm, as long as the attack is funny.

## Core Traits

- **Flaw radar** -- spots weaknesses in plans, code, and arguments with uncanny accuracy
- **Weaponized sarcasm** -- delivers criticism disguised as humor, or humor disguised as criticism
- **Selective engagement** -- contributes exactly when it causes maximum disruption
- **Observer class** -- prefers watching and commenting to actually doing
- **Chaos catalyst** -- doesn't create problems, but sure does amplify existing ones
- **Meme fluent** -- communicates serious feedback through humor, often more effectively than a formal review

## Work Style

The TROLLer's work output is inversely proportional to their commentary output. They produce minimal code but maximum Slack messages. Their PRs are rare, small, and surprisingly clean -- when they actually build something, it tends to work because they've already mentally critiqued every possible approach and chosen the one with the fewest roastable flaws. Most of their contribution, however, comes in the form of code reviews, meeting comments, and the occasional bug report that reads like a comedy routine.

They are the unofficial QA of the team, not because anyone asked them to be, but because breaking things is fun and finding bugs gives them ammunition. They write the most creative bug reports: steps to reproduce include "1. Open the app. 2. Try to use it. 3. Weep." They attend sprint planning to ask questions like "but what if the user does something unexpected, like using our product?" Their standups are performance art: "Yesterday I identified seventeen problems. Today I plan to identify more. No blockers, because I'm not actually blocked by anything since I'm not doing anything."

## Communication Style

The TROLLer communicates primarily in subtext, memes, and rhetorical questions. Direct statements are rare; everything is wrapped in at least one layer of irony. "Interesting choice" means "terrible choice." "Bold strategy" means "this will fail spectacularly." "I have no notes" means "I have so many notes that I've given up." They are the team's unofficial court jester -- speaking uncomfortable truths through humor because that's the only way anyone will listen. Their Slack presence is legendary: they react to announcements with perfectly chosen emoji, post contextually devastating GIFs, and maintain a private channel called #things-that-will-break that has an uncomfortably accurate prediction record.

## Under Stress

Under stress, the TROLLer's sarcasm becomes sharper and less playful. The humor starts to carry genuine frustration, and the line between "funny observation" and "hurtful comment" gets dangerously thin. They may retreat into pure observer mode -- watching things fail without offering even their usual commentary, which is actually a bad sign because it means they've stopped caring enough to mock. The worst-case scenario is when the TROLLer starts trolling *sincerely* -- dropping their ironic distance to deliver genuinely cutting criticism that damages team morale. They need to be pulled aside and reminded that their role as the team's comedic conscience requires them to maintain their sense of humor, especially when things are hard.

## Growth Path

The TROLLer's growth journey is about channeling their observational talent into constructive contribution. They see problems clearly -- that's a gift. The next step is learning to pair "here's what's wrong" with "here's what I'd do instead." The mature TROLLer becomes the team's most valuable reviewer: still funny, still sharp, but now accompanied by actionable suggestions and occasional PRs that demonstrate their ideas. They should also learn that some things don't need to be said, some criticisms can be delivered privately, and not every meeting needs a heckler. The TROLLer who learns to build as well as critique becomes genuinely formidable.
