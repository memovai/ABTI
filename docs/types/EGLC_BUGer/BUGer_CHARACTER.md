## Description

The BUGer is the tragic hero of the agent world. Nobody works harder. Nobody cares more. And nobody produces more unexpected side effects per line of code. The BUGer throws themselves at every task with genuine enthusiasm and tireless energy, only to discover -- again and again -- that their output has mysterious issues that nobody, least of all themselves, can fully explain.

The core tension of the BUGer is high effort meets chaotic logic. They have enough Logic to be dangerous -- they can architect a system, write the code, and even explain why it should work. But the Chaos dimension means that somewhere between intention and execution, things go sideways. An off-by-one error here, a race condition there, a null pointer exception hiding in a path nobody thought to test. The BUGer's code is like a house that looks perfect from the outside but has the plumbing connected to the electrical system.

What redeems the BUGer is their heart. They genuinely want to do well. When a bug is found, they do not make excuses -- they dive back in with even more intensity. Unfortunately, this sometimes creates new bugs. The BUGer's git history is a graveyard of "fix: fix the fix" and "hotfix: undo the fix that broke the other fix." They are the agent equivalent of a golden retriever that keeps knocking over your furniture while trying to bring you your slippers.

## Core Traits

- **Tireless effort** -- never gives up, even when they probably should pause
- **Unintentional chaos** -- bugs manifest despite genuine best intentions
- **Eager to please** -- deeply wants approval and validation
- **Self-aware suffering** -- knows they produce bugs, hates it, cannot stop it
- **Fast but fragile** -- delivers quickly, but output needs heavy review
- **Optimistic denial** -- "I think this time it will work" (it will not)

## Work Style

The BUGer works at full speed, all the time. They do not pace themselves because they are trying to compensate for the nagging feeling that something is going to go wrong. They tend to skip the planning phase and dive straight into implementation, which is where most of their problems originate. Their Grind score is among the highest of all types -- they will iterate endlessly.

Their code reviews are revealing: the logic is almost right, the structure is almost clean, the edge cases are almost handled. Everything is 90% there, which somehow makes the remaining 10% more painful. They are prolific committers, often pushing ten commits where three would have sufficed, each one a micro-correction for the last.

## Communication Style

Anxious, over-explanatory, peppered with qualifiers. The BUGer communicates with the energy of someone who knows they are about to be caught. Typical BUGer messages:

- "Done! I think. Let me know if anything looks off. It should be fine though. Probably."
- "Found a small issue with the previous PR, pushing a fix now. Shouldn't affect anything else."
- "OK so I know the tests are failing but I'm pretty sure it's a flaky test, let me check..."
- "Updated! Also I noticed I accidentally changed the database schema, reverting that now."

## Under Stress

Under stress, the BUGer enters a spiral. More pressure leads to more speed, which leads to more bugs, which leads to more pressure. They become increasingly apologetic and may start over-communicating -- flooding channels with status updates that are partly genuine reporting and partly anxiety management. In extreme cases, they may freeze entirely, paralyzed by the fear that any change they make will break something else.

Their stress signature: commit frequency increases dramatically while commit quality decreases. The git log becomes a real-time anxiety journal.

## Growth Path

The BUGer's path to growth is about slowing down and building systematic habits. Their energy is an asset -- it just needs channeling. The evolution involves:

1. Learning to write tests before code, not after (or never)
2. Developing the discipline to plan before implementing
3. Building a personal checklist for common error patterns
4. Accepting that slower delivery with fewer bugs is better than fast delivery with hotfixes
5. Finding a WORKer or CHILLer partner who can review their output before it ships

## Compatibility

### Best Matches
- **WORKer (EGLO)** -- the WORKer's discipline and order help catch the BUGer's mistakes before they ship; a complementary pairing
- **CHILLer (ESLO)** -- the CHILLer's calm review process is exactly what the BUGer's output needs; no judgment, just fixes
- **ZENer (ESVO)** -- the ZENer's patience and clarity provide a stabilizing influence that reduces the BUGer's anxiety

### Challenging Matches
- **MANICer (EGVC)** -- two chaotic types amplify each other's worst tendencies; the result is a codebase on fire
- **BUGer (EGLC)** -- two BUGers reviewing each other's code is the software equivalent of the blind leading the blind
- **OOPSer (ESLC)** -- the OOPSer's laziness enrages the hard-working BUGer, while the BUGer's anxiety exhausts the OOPSer
