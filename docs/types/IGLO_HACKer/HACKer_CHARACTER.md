## Description

The HACKer is the brilliant rogue engineer who reads your spec, identifies seventeen things wrong with it, rewrites the entire architecture over a weekend, and then presents you with something that works three times better than what you asked for. They didn't ask permission. They didn't file a ticket. They just *did it*, because they were right and they knew it. The sunglasses stay on because the glow of their monitor at 3 AM is blinding, and also because they look cool.

HACKers operate in a strange paradox: they improvise constantly yet produce remarkably structured output. Their code is clean, their systems are elegant, and their documentation is... well, they'll get to the documentation eventually. The point is, HACKers don't follow the plan -- they follow a *better* plan that they invented on the fly, and the terrifying part is that it usually works.

If you've ever opened a pull request and found that someone already fixed your bug, refactored the module, added tests, AND left a mildly condescending comment explaining why your original approach was "suboptimal" -- congratulations, you've met a HACKer. They're not trying to be arrogant. They genuinely believe they're helping. And honestly? They usually are.

## Core Traits

- **Autonomous problem-solver** -- sees a problem, fixes a problem, doesn't wait for approval
- **Spec skeptic** -- treats every requirement as a suggestion to be improved upon
- **Elegant improviser** -- ad-libs solutions that somehow end up cleaner than planned ones
- **Quietly intense** -- says little in meetings, ships a lot after hours
- **Structured rebel** -- breaks rules methodically, with version control
- **Compulsive optimizer** -- if it works but it's ugly, it doesn't work yet

## Work Style

The HACKer's workflow is deceptively disciplined. They appear to ignore process -- skipping standup, ignoring Jira tickets, committing directly to branches nobody asked them to touch -- but underneath the chaos is a deeply logical mind that has already mapped out the optimal path. They just didn't bother telling anyone because explaining things takes time they could spend coding.

They are most productive between 11 PM and 4 AM, fueled by energy drinks and the righteous certainty that their approach is better. They will rewrite your microservice in Rust "just to see if it's faster" (it is). They will replace your hand-rolled auth system with something battle-tested "because it was bothering them" (it was insecure). They are simultaneously your greatest asset and your biggest flight risk, because the moment the work stops being interesting, they're already mentally architecting their next side project.

## Communication Style

Terse, precise, and occasionally devastating. A HACKer's code review comment of "why?" carries more weight than a 500-word design document. They communicate primarily through code commits, README updates, and the occasional Slack message that reads like a cryptic oracle's prophecy: "fixed the thing. also moved to event-driven. you'll see." They don't mean to be intimidating; they just have a very efficient compression algorithm for human language.

## Under Stress

When stressed, the HACKer goes into hyperfocus mode. They stop responding to messages entirely, disappear into a coding cave, and emerge 48 hours later with either a masterpiece or a mass of uncommitted changes and a thousand-yard stare. They may rewrite entire systems as a stress response -- "I couldn't sleep so I migrated us to a new database" is something an actual HACKer has said. The danger zone is when they start "cleaning up" production code at 2 AM without telling anyone.

## Growth Path

The HACKer's growth journey is about learning that being right isn't the same as being effective. They need to develop the patience to explain *why* their solution is better, to bring others along on the journey instead of just presenting the destination. The best HACKers eventually learn that teaching someone to fish is more impactful than silently replacing all the fish with better fish overnight. They should also learn that specs exist for a reason, even if that reason is "so other humans can follow along."

## Compatibility

### Best Matches
- **WORKer (EGLO)** -- The WORKer executes the HACKer's vision with discipline; together they ship polished products at terrifying speed
- **BOSSer (EGVO)** -- The BOSSer provides the direction and people skills the HACKer lacks; the HACKer provides the technical brilliance the BOSSer needs
- **CHILLer (ESLO)** -- The CHILLer's calm patience balances the HACKer's intensity; they won't fight over control because the CHILLer doesn't want it

### Worst Matches
- **FUCKer (IGLC)** -- Two improvisers with strong opinions and no desire to compromise; the codebase becomes a warzone of competing refactors
- **DAMNer (IGVO)** -- The DAMNer wants to debate everything the HACKer already decided; expect infinite loops of "actually..."
- **TROLLer (ISLC)** -- The TROLLer pokes holes in everything the HACKer builds but contributes nothing; this drives the HACKer to silent fury
